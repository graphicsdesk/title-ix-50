Administration

1983: Columbia-Barnard Athletic Consortium created

Photo courtesy of Columbia Daily Spectator Archives

Barnard and Columbia made history in 1983 with the creation of the Columbia-Barnard Athletic Consortium. This unprecedented endeavor allowed a women’s college, Barnard, to compete in the NCAA for the first time. The program has since expanded, especially under Barnard President Sian Beilock. During her tenure, the number of athletes at Barnard has grown exponentially. – Jorge Hernandez

2004: M. Dianne Murphy appointed as athletic director

After a successful stint serving as the director of athletics and recreation at the University of Denver, M. Dianne Murphy was appointed as Columbia’s athletic director by University President Lee Bollinger in 2004. Her 11-year tenure saw 30 Columbia teams receive Ivy League titles. Perhaps her most notable achievement was the Columbia Campaign for Athletics: Achieving Excellence. As the most successful fundraising campaign in Columbia Athletics history, it helped raise over $100 million for the program. – Jorge Hernandez

2007: Columbia Athletics Women’s Leadership Council established

In 2007, M. Dianne Murphy and several alumnae founded the Columbia Women’s Leadership Council, a “leadership-giving recognition society for women.” Since its creation, the council has raised over $7.5 million for Columbia women’s athletics. The program aims to create ties between athletic alumnae and current athletes. – Miles Schachner

Archery

Photo courtesy of Columbia Daily Spectator Archives

1980s: Columbia archery has undefeated run

In the 1980s, the women’s archery program at Columbia was a force to be reckoned with. “We are probably the greatest athletic team of Columbia University,” Al Lizzio, longtime coach of the women’s archery team, told Spectator in 1986. The team went undefeated until near the end of the decade, when it fell in its season opener against Baruch College in 1989, a narrow loss of 548-543. – Heather Chen

Basketball

1985-86: Nancy Kalafus leads women’s basketball to historic season

The 1985-86 season was just the second season of women’s basketball. Nancy Kalafus, then the head coach of the Lions, led the team to the winningest season in program history. The 21-6 record, achieved in Division III, paved the way for the Lions’ subsequent play in Division I. The team won third place in the NCAA tournament that year with an 89-73 victory over Buffalo State College. Kalafus would coach the Lions for an additional five seasons before her departure in 1991. – Adam Lang

1987: Ula Lysniak sets women’s basketball career record

As the first Columbia woman to score 1000 points in her career, Ula Lysniak, BC ’87, is one of Columbia basketball’s all-time greats. Lysniak started every game in her four years on the team and is one of the Lions’ all-time leading scorers with 1,447 points. She also added 764 rebounds in her career. After graduating from Barnard in 1987, Lysniak played in Austria as the first Light Blue woman player to ever play professionally. – Rebecca Wachen

Megan Griffith’s career, from Columbia player to head coach

Photo courtesy of Columbia Daily Spectator Archives

Currently in her seventh season as head coach at her alma mater, Megan Griffith, CC ’07, has transformed Columbia women’s basketball into a team that sits near the top of the Ivy League. In her first six seasons, she led the Lions to a 0.414 win rate against Ivy League teams, including a 0.857 Ivy win rate in 2021-22. During her own collegiate career, Griffith scored over 1000 points, sitting at eighth on the Columbia all-time scoring leaderboard. Named the 2021-22 CBB Analytics Women’s Basketball Coach of the Year by the Asian Coaches Association, Griffith looks to keep building momentum as she continues shaping Columbia women’s basketball into a national powerhouse. – Rebecca Wachen

Cross Country

2002-05: Women’s cross country wins Ivy League Heptagonal Championship four years in a row

During the 2002-05 season, the women’s cross country team was untouchable, defending its status as Ivy League champion for four years. During that reign, the Lions would claim back-to-back individual Heps Championship titles with Caroline Bierbaum, CC ’06, who won it all in 2004 and 2005. Her feat was so impressive that her 2005 5k time still tops Columbia’s record books at 17:01.4. – Alexander Kwok

Fencing

Photo courtesy of Columbia Daily Spectator Archives

1987: Katy Bilodeau repeats as individual national champion

In 1985, Katy Bilodeau, CC ’87, won the NCAA national title in women’s foil. In 1987, she did it again, becoming the first female fencer to win two NCAA titles. The Columbia Athletics Hall of Famer finished her career as one of the most decorated Lions ever. Among many other honors, she was the first fencer to be a four-time first-team All-American, the 1980s NCAA Athlete of the Decade in fencing, and a two-time Olympian in 1988 and 1992. – Robert Gao

1992: First combined national title, three years after NCAA integrates fencing scoring

When Columbia won the 1992 NCAA championship, it was the program’s 12th of all time. More importantly though, it was the Lions’ first since the national fencing championships adopted a co-ed model, coming just three years after the NCAA switched to the combined men’s and women’s scoring format and less than a decade after Columbia itself began admitting women in 1983. Three-time Olympian Anne Marsh, CC ’94, played a key role, finishing second in women’s foil with a 14-2 record. Then the Lions won it all again, repeating as national champions in 1993 despite coming in as the underdog. Marsh again put up strong numbers, placing third with a 16-1 record. –Robert Gao

2005: Emily Jacobson wins individual title as first-year

After competing at the 2004 Olympics, Emily Jacobson, CC ’08, exploded onto the national fencing scene in her rookie year, winning the 2005 NCAA sabre title and becoming Columbia’s first female athlete to win an individual championship since 1990. It was a great start to an illustrious national career, as Jacobson would never finish lower than third at the NCAA Championships. As a result, she became one of two female fencers at Columbia to be a four-time first-team All-American. –Robert Gao

2008: Erinn Smart’s Columbia career and Olympic medal

Erinn Smart, BC ’01, made Columbia history on a global stage. The two-time All-American fencer, who won four national championships, became Columbia’s first Black Olympic medalist when the United States beat Hungary to earn a silver medal. – Miles Schachner

Field Hockey

Photo courtesy of Columbia Daily Spectator Archives

2007: Gena Miller becomes Columbia field hockey’s first All-American

After starting as goalkeeper in all 17 games in 2007, Gena Miller, CC ’08, became the first Columbia field hockey player to receive All-American recognition on the third team and achieved first-team All-Ivy honors. Miller had a stellar season, earning the most save percentage in the country with .835. The only other Columbia field hockey player to receive All-American honors is midfielder Christina Freibott, CC ’15, in 2014. – Simrin Ponamgi

Golf

Photo courtesy of Columbia Athletics

2007: Ivy team champions and individual women’s winner

During the 2007 Ivy League championship, Columbia earned a score of 933, won the women’s bracket, and became the first Ivy other than Yale or Princeton to take a league title. As the individual Ivy League champion, Sara Ovadia, CC ’09, led the Lions to their first team title in program history. Ovadia’s success was not a surprise, given her impressive golfing resume and the California native’s status as No. 24 in the US for Women’s Amateurs. – Alexander Kwok

Lacrosse

1998: Sara Brubaker beats Notre Dame with last second goal

Photo courtesy of Columbia Daily Spectator Archives

During the 1998 women’s lacrosse season, the new Columbia program was tied with Notre Dame with only 30 seconds left on the clock. The Irish defense was called for a foul, and Sara Brubaker, BC ’00, stepped up to take an 8-meter shot. With the game on the line, Brubaker bounced the ball past the Irish goalie. Brubaker would go on to captain the team in the 1999 and 2000 seasons, with this game-winner against the Irish setting the tone for the rest of her lacrosse career. - Sydney Stoller

2003: Adie Moll’s single-season lacrosse record

Columbia women’s lacrosse struggled in its first few seasons as a varsity sport, but a bright spot during this time was Adie Moll, CC ’04. In 2003, Moll was awarded second-team All Ivy status. That season, she set a single-season and school record in scoring, boosting team morale and providing hope forthe young program. A senior co-captain in 2004, Moll continued her scoring spree and netted five goals in the season opener that helped the Lions rally and beat Wagner College in overtime. – Sydney Stoller

Rowing

2004: Stacey Borgman wins women’s lightweight double skulls at the Olympics in Athens

Photo courtesy of Columbia Athletics

Stacey Borgman, BC ’98, began her path to greatness during her sophomore year at Barnard when, at the request of her mother, she walked onto the rowing team, even though she had never set foot in a scull. What followed was an illustrious career, where she won nine national titles at the U.S. Rowing National Championships. However, the highlight of her career came in 2004, when she competed for the U.S. women’s rowing team in Athens at the Olympics. As the first Columbia women’s rowing alumnus to compete in the Olympics, Borgman won the women’s lightweight double sculls. She was subsequently inducted into the Columbia Athletics Hall of Fame in 2008, cementing her impressive legacy. – Lucas Metzger

2008: Rowing team beats Cornell by one tenth of a second

Columbia women’s rowing defied expectations during Cornell’s Dunn Bowl Regatta in 2008. With only 500 meters to go in the race, Cornell maintained a lead of at least half a length on the Lions. However, the Lions kept fighting, managing to draw even with the Cornell boat and then pull ahead to finish second with a record of 6:36, one tenth of a second faster than Cornell’s time, an incredibly close finish. – Lucas Metzger

Soccer

1991: Rachel Barney, CC ’93, saves 34 shots in match against Cornell

On Sept. 14, 1991, Columbia goalkeeper Rachel Barney saved a whopping 34 shots against Cornell, shattering an Ivy League record. Over 30 years later, no other Ivy League goalkeeper has cracked 30 single-game saves. Barney’s 34 saves is the second most in NCAA Division I women’s soccer history. – Julian Silva-Forbes

2006: Women’s soccer wins Ivy League championship with undefeated season

Columbia women’s soccer won its only Ivy League championship in 2006, adding a rare win to the Lions’ arsenal. The team was undefeated in league games and was the only Ivy League team to enter the NCAA tournament. The seven seniors on the team exemplified leadership and cooperation for younger players and set the tone for their successful season. – Sydney Stoller

Photo courtesy of Columbia Daily Spectator Archives

Softball

2002: Softball posts ERA of 1.60, record wins, runs, walks

In 2002, softball pitchers ended the season with a team ERA of 1.60, the lowest in Columbia’s history. Headed by ace Allison Buehler, CC ’03, who put up the second-lowest individual ERA in program history with 1.26, beaten only by her 0.78 ERA from the previous season, the team gave up just 110 runs with 80 earned and 47 walks en route to 27 wins on the season, all records for the team. – Brian Lee

Swimming & Diving

1998: Cristina Teuscher, CC ’00, wins individual events at NCAA Championship

Cristina Teuscher, CC ’00, won the 500-meter freestyle and 400-meter intermediate medley at the 1998 NCAA Championship. Teuscher remains the lone Lion to win an NCAA championship in swimming and diving, and she would go on to win two more in 2000. Teuscher won a gold medal in the 4x200m freestyle in the 1996 Summer Olympics. – Owen Tesoro

Volleyball

2000: Volleyball has best season ever

On Nov. 10, 2000, volleyball put together the program’s best all-around performance during an Ivy League tournament playoff game versus Princeton. Despite falling in three hard-fought sets, the team posted record highs in kills, assists, and digs with 110, 106, and 140, respectively. The Lions also posted their second-highest service aces in a single match with 22 in just three sets. – Brian Lee
